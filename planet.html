Terrestrial Planets

Terrestrial planets are rocky worlds primarily composed of silicate rocks or metals. These planets are characterized by their solid surfaces, relatively small sizes, and proximity to the Sun in our solar system.

Mercury

The smallest and closest planet to the Sun

Venus

Similar in size to Earth with a toxic atmosphere

Earth

Our home planet, the only known world with life

Mars

The red planet with potential for future human exploration

Gas Giants

Gas giants are large planets primarily composed of hydrogen and helium. These planets lack a solid surface and are much larger and more massive than terrestrial planets.

Mercury

Mercury is the smallest and innermost planet in the Solar System. It has no natural satellites and no substantial atmosphere. The planet has a large iron core which generates a magnetic field about 1% as strong as Earth's. Its surface is heavily cratered, resembling Earth's Moon.

Venus

Venus is the second planet from the Sun and is Earth's closest planetary neighbor. It's one of the four inner, terrestrial planets, and it's often called Earth's twin because it's similar in size and density. Venus has a thick, toxic atmosphere filled with carbon dioxide and clouds of sulfuric acid.

Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only astronomical object known to harbor life. About 29.2% of Earth's surface is land, and the remaining 70.8% is covered with water. Earth's atmosphere consists of 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, and 1% other gases which helps protect the planet from harmful solar radiation.

Mars

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun and the second-smallest planet in the Solar System. Mars is often called the "Red Planet" due to its reddish appearance, which is caused by iron oxide (rust) on its surface. Mars has two small moons, Phobos and Deimos, which may be captured asteroids.

Jupiter

Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun and the largest in the Solar System. It is a gas giant primarily composed of hydrogen, but helium comprises one-quarter of its mass. Jupiter's best-known feature is the Great Red Spot, a persistent anticyclonic storm larger than Earth.

Saturn

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and the second-largest in the Solar System, after Jupiter. It is a gas giant with an average radius of about nine times that of Earth. Saturn is known for its prominent ring system, which consists of ice particles, rocky debris, and dust.
